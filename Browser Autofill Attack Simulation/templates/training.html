Introduction to Phishing

15 minutes

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyber attack where criminals impersonate legitimate organizations to steal sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, and personal data. Attackers create fake websites, emails, and messages that look authentic to trick victims into revealing their credentials.

Why Phishing Works

Phishing succeeds because it exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities:

  • Trust: We trust familiar brands and designs
  • Urgency: "Act now or lose your account!" creates panic
  • Authority: Messages appearing from bosses or banks command action
  • Familiarity: Emails look identical to real communications
  • Curiosity: "You've won a prize!" triggers investigation

üìß Real-World Example

In 2016, phishing emails targeted Gmail users with fake Google login pages. Over 1 million credentials were stolen before Google blocked the attack. The pages looked identical to real Google - same logo, colors, and layout.

Types of Phishing Attacks

  • Email Phishing: Mass emails pretending to be from banks, services, etc.
  • Spear Phishing: Targeted attacks on specific individuals with personalized info
  • Whaling: Attacks targeting executives and high-value targets
  • Smishing: Phishing via SMS text messages
  • Vishing: Voice phishing via phone calls

Impact on Businesses

Phishing attacks cost businesses an average of $14.8 million per year. Beyond financial loss, companies face data breaches, regulatory fines, reputation damage, and loss of customer trust.

Browser Autofill Risks

20 minutes

How Browser Autofill Works

Modern browsers offer autofill to save time by automatically filling forms with saved information:

  • Usernames & Passwords: Stored in browser password manager
  • Personal Information: Name, address, phone number
  • Payment Data: Credit card numbers, expiration dates
  • Form Data: Email addresses, search history

The Security Vulnerability

Browsers autofill based on simple pattern matching - they look for input fields with names like "email", "password", "username". Attackers exploit this by creating fake pages with these same field names. When you visit the fake page, your browser automatically fills in your real credentials!

‚ö†Ô∏è The Hidden Danger

Autofill happens instantly when you load the page - you don't even need to click anything! Attackers can capture your credentials using invisible forms or JavaScript before you realize what's happening.

Exploitation Techniques

Attackers use several methods to steal autofilled data:

  • Invisible Fields: Hidden form fields that autofill but aren't visible
  • JavaScript Capture: Scripts that read field values immediately on page load
  • Fake Submit Buttons: Buttons that look real but send data to attackers
  • iFrame Tricks: Embedding real site login in invisible frame

üíª Technical Example

Simple JavaScript that captures autofilled data:

<input type="email" name="email" autocomplete="username">
<input type="password" name="password" autocomplete="current-password">
<script>
  // Captures data 500ms after page loads
  setTimeout(() => {
    const email = document.querySelector('[name="email"]').value;
    const password = document.querySelector('[name="password"]').value;
    sendToAttacker(email, password);
  }, 500);
</script>

Browser Security Settings

You can protect yourself by configuring your browser:

  • Chrome: Settings ‚Üí Autofill ‚Üí Passwords ‚Üí Disable "Offer to save"
  • Firefox: Settings ‚Üí Privacy ‚Üí Logins and Passwords ‚Üí Uncheck autofill
  • Safari: Preferences ‚Üí AutoFill ‚Üí Uncheck "User names and passwords"
  • Edge: Settings ‚Üí Profiles ‚Üí Passwords ‚Üí Disable password saving

Social Engineering Tactics

25 minutes

The Psychology of Deception

Social engineering attacks manipulate human emotions and cognitive biases to bypass security:

  • Fear: "Your account will be deleted!"
  • Greed: "You've won $10,000!"
  • Curiosity: "See who viewed your profile"
  • Helpfulness: "Your colleague needs help urgently"
  • Authority: "CEO requests immediate action"

Urgency and Fear Tactics

Attackers create artificial time pressure to prevent careful thinking. Common phrases include:

  • "Unusual activity detected - verify now!"
  • "Account suspended - click within 24 hours"
  • "Payment failed - update immediately"
  • "Security alert - confirm your identity"

üìß Example Phishing Email

"Dear Customer,

We've detected suspicious activity on your PayPal account. Someone attempted to login from Russia. Your account has been temporarily locked for security.

Click here within 24 hours to verify your identity or your account will be permanently closed.

Thank you,
PayPal Security Team"

Red Flags: Creates urgency, threatens account closure, generic greeting, suspicious link.

Authority Impersonation

Attackers pretend to be authority figures to compel obedience:

  • Brand Authority: Impersonating Google, Microsoft, Apple
  • Organizational Authority: Fake emails from CEO or IT department
  • Government Authority: Pretending to be IRS, police, court
  • Technical Authority: Fake "IT support" calls

Pretexting Techniques

Creating elaborate fake scenarios to gain trust:

  • Researching target on social media for personalization
  • Using real employee names found on LinkedIn
  • Referencing actual company events or projects
  • Spoofing phone numbers and email addresses

Defense Strategies

  • Slow Down: Never act immediately on urgent requests
  • Verify Independently: Contact the organization directly using known numbers
  • Question Authority: It's okay to verify even from "bosses"
  • Trust Your Instincts: If something feels off, it probably is
  • Report Suspicious Messages: Forward to IT or security team

Detection Techniques

30 minutes

URL Analysis

The URL is your first line of defense. Learn to read URLs from right to left:

  • Real: https://accounts.google.com/signin
  • Fake: https://accounts-google.com/signin (missing dot!)
  • Fake: https://google.com.verify-account.net (real domain is .net)
  • Fake: https://goog1e.com/signin (number "1" instead of letter "l")

üîç Pro Tip: Read Right to Left

In "accounts.google.com", reading right to left: ".com" is the TLD, "google" is the domain, "accounts" is the subdomain. In "google.com.phishing.net", the real domain is "phishing.net"!

SSL Certificate Verification

Steps to check SSL certificate:

  1. Click the padlock icon in the address bar
  2. Select "Certificate" or "Connection is secure"
  3. Verify the certificate is issued to the correct domain
  4. Check the issuing authority (should be known CA like DigiCert, Let's Encrypt)
  5. Verify expiration date is current

Email Header Inspection

Email headers reveal the true sender. Look for:

  • Return-Path: Should match the claimed sender domain
  • Received: Shows the path the email took (look for suspicious servers)
  • SPF/DKIM/DMARC: Authentication results (PASS is good)
  • Reply-To: Should match sender, if different it's suspicious

Design Inconsistencies

Phishing pages often have subtle flaws:

  • Blurry or pixelated logos
  • Spelling and grammar errors
  • Misaligned elements or broken layouts
  • Missing footer information
  • Unusual fonts or colors
  • Generic placeholder text

Red Flags Checklist

Before entering credentials, verify ALL of these:

  • ‚òê URL is exactly correct (no typos, extra characters)
  • ‚òê SSL certificate is valid and matches domain
  • ‚òê Design looks professional (no blurry images)
  • ‚òê No spelling or grammar errors
  • ‚òê Not feeling rushed or pressured
  • ‚òê Expected this login request
  • ‚òê Requesting appropriate information only

If ANY box is unchecked, DO NOT enter credentials!

Protection Methods

25 minutes

Password Manager Setup

Password managers provide the best protection because they verify the domain before autofilling. Recommended options:

  • 1Password: Cross-platform, family sharing, travel mode
  • Bitwarden: Open-source, free tier available, self-hosting option
  • LastPass: User-friendly interface, browser integration
  • Dashlane: Includes VPN, dark web monitoring

Why password managers help against phishing:

  • Only autofill on exact domain matches (won't fill on goog1e.com)
  • Generate strong, unique passwords for each site
  • Encrypted storage protects credentials
  • Alert you of weak or reused passwords
  • Notify if credentials found in data breaches

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even if phishing steals your password, 2FA provides a second security layer. Methods ranked by security:

  1. Hardware Security Keys (Best): YubiKey, Google Titan - Physical devices, phishing-resistant
  2. Authenticator Apps (Good): Google Authenticator, Authy - Time-based codes
  3. SMS Codes (Acceptable): Better than nothing, but vulnerable to SIM swapping
  4. Email Codes (Weakest): If email is compromised, 2FA is bypassed

üí° Enable 2FA On These Accounts

  • Email accounts (Gmail, Outlook)
  • Financial services (banks, PayPal)
  • Social media (Facebook, Instagram)
  • Cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox)
  • Work accounts (Office 365, Slack)
  • Password manager itself
  • Cryptocurrency exchanges

Security Software

Additional protection layers:

  • Antivirus: Real-time malware and phishing site blocking
  • Browser Extensions: Netcraft, uBlock Origin for phishing detection
  • DNS Filtering: Cloudflare 1.1.1.1, Quad9 block malicious sites
  • Email Filters: Gmail/Outlook have built-in phishing detection

Incident Response

If you suspect you've been phished:

  1. Change Passwords Immediately: For the affected account and any using the same password
  2. Enable 2FA: If not already active
  3. Check Account Activity: Look for unauthorized logins or changes
  4. Contact the Company: Report the incident to the legitimate organization
  5. Monitor Financial Accounts: Watch for unauthorized transactions
  6. Report to Authorities: File report with IC3.gov or local cybercrime unit
  7. Inform Your Organization: If work-related, notify IT/security team

Advanced Threats

35 minutes

Spear Phishing

Unlike mass phishing, spear phishing targets specific individuals with personalized information. Attackers research victims on social media, company websites, and LinkedIn to create convincing messages.

üéØ Real Attack Example

"Hi Sarah,

I saw your presentation at the AWS Summit last week - great insights on cloud migration! I'm reaching out because I'm working on a similar project for Acme Corp.

Could you review this proposal document? [malicious link]

Thanks,
John from TechStart"

Why it works: References real event, knows victim's name and company, appears relevant to victim's work.

Whaling Attacks

"Whaling" targets high-value individuals like CEOs, CFOs, and executives. These attacks often involve business email compromise (BEC) where attackers impersonate executives to authorize wire transfers or access sensitive data.

  • CEO Fraud: Fake urgent emails from "CEO" requesting wire transfer
  • W-2 Scams: Fake HR requests for employee tax information
  • Vendor Fraud: Compromised vendor emails with changed payment details

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

BEC attacks cost businesses over $2.4 billion annually. Common scenarios:

  • Invoice Fraud: Fake invoices with attacker's bank details
  • Payroll Diversion: Requests to change direct deposit information
  • Data Theft: Requesting W-2s or customer data
  • Attorney Impersonation: Fake legal requests for confidential info

‚ö†Ô∏è Defense: Multi-Step Verification

Always verify unusual requests through a second channel. If you receive an email from your CEO requesting a wire transfer, call their office directly using a known number - don't reply to the email or use contact info provided in the message!

Man-in-the-Middle Attacks

MITM attacks intercept communication between user and legitimate service:

  • Public WiFi Attacks: Fake "Free WiFi" hotspots capture traffic
  • SSL Stripping: Downgrading HTTPS to HTTP to intercept data
  • DNS Spoofing: Redirecting legitimate domains to attacker servers

Protection: Use VPN on public networks, verify HTTPS always active, enable DNS-over-HTTPS

Zero-Day Exploits

Zero-day attacks exploit unknown vulnerabilities before patches are available:

  • Browser Exploits: Malicious code executing through browser vulnerabilities
  • Plugin Vulnerabilities: Compromised browser extensions or plugins
  • Email Client Exploits: Malware delivered through email reader bugs

Defense Strategy:

  • Keep all software updated with latest patches
  • Use browser sandboxing features
  • Disable unnecessary plugins
  • Use endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools
  • Implement network segmentation

Emerging Threats

  • AI-Generated Phishing: ChatGPT creating convincing phishing content
  • Deepfake Voice: AI-cloned voices in vishing attacks
  • QR Code Phishing: Malicious QR codes redirecting to fake sites
  • Cryptocurrency Scams: Fake wallet sites stealing crypto
